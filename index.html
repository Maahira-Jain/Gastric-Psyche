A personal research system for an emerging subfield — Gastropsychology — studying how eating behaviour constructs identity, encodes morality, and archives culture. This is a thinking tool, not a publication. Everything here exists to help clarify, track, and deepen the work.

Core Concept

Every school of psychology has treated food as a symptom — a behaviour to manage, a craving to regulate, a disorder to diagnose. This research programme proposes the opposite: food is not a symptom of the self, but its primary construction material. Before we had language for who we are, we had food rituals that made us who we are.

The specific focus is three-part: how food encodes moral identity, how it preserves and transmits cultural memory, and how digital food environments are now disrupting both — in ways psychology has not yet measured.

The proposed subfield — Gastropsychology — sits at the intersection of moral psychology, cultural anthropology, embodied cognition, and digital behaviour. India is the primary field site: the most food-morally complex society on earth, and almost completely absent from the Western psychology literature.

Researcher Positioning

I am a psychologist working at the boundary where the body meets culture. My research treats eating not as a health behaviour but as a philosophical act — one that encodes identity, rehearses morality, and archives civilisation. I am building the foundations of a subfield that takes seriously what every grandmother and every anthropologist already knows, but what experimental psychology has barely touched.
